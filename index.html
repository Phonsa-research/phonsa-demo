Phonsa: Automatic Phoneme Annotation, Segmentation, and Breath Detection for Singing Voice Synthesis

Submitted to ACML 2025

Explore Research

Abstract

Precise phoneme-level and breath annotations are crucial for training high-quality Singing Voice Synthesis (SVS) models. However, obtaining such annotations typically requires labor-intensive manual labeling or relies on automatic methods with notable limitations. Existing approaches often struggle with fine-grained force alignment in singing voice, lack integrated breath detection capabilities, or fail to perform unsupervised phoneme segmentation in the absence of lyrics. To address these challenges, we propose Phonsa, an automatic annotation system for singing voice. Built upon a pretrained Whisper encoder-decoder architecture, Phonsa incorporates a chunked self-attention classifier for phoneme-level prediction, introduces an explicit breath class with a dedicated processing strategy for automatic breath annotation, and enables unsupervised phoneme segmentation through a novel de-coding algorithm. In addition, a specially designed boundary token is employed to improve the separation of consecutive identical phonemes, thereby enhancing both breath detection and segmentation accuracy. We evaluate Phonsa on Chinese and Japanese singing data, demonstrating its superior performance over traditional methods in forced alignment, as well as its novel capabilities in automatic breath detection and unsupervised phoneme segmentation. Importantly, SVS models trained with Phonsa-generated annotations achieve mentation. Importantly, SVS models trained with Phonsa-generated annotations achieve significantly higher perceptual quality in subjective Mean Opinion Score (MOS) evaluations compared to those trained with MFA-based annotations. The Phonsa system, along with pretrained Chinese and Japanese models, will be released as an open-source toolkit.

Experimental Results

The following visualizations are plotted using Praat (https://www.fon.hum.uva.nl/praat). In figures, "#" indicates silence, "@" indicates breath events, and "GT" represents the ground truth label.

Task 1: Alignment Results

The figures below compare phoneme alignment accuracy between MFA and Phonsa on singing voice data. Phonsa achieves significantly better alignment precision, especially for challenging singing characteristics like vibrato and breath sounds. GT is the ground truth label.

Alignment Example 1, Chinese

In the first half, Phonsa demonstrates alignment accuracy comparable to that of MFA. However, in the latter half, MFA's alignment becomes noticeably less accurate, revealing its limitations in handling more complex segments. In contrast, Phonsa successfully identifies the precise locations of breath events (marked with the @ symbol), which MFA can not detect.

Alignment Example 2, Chinese

This example illustrates that MFA can be confused by homophones, even when there is a large gap between the two identical phonemes. Such confusion often causes subsequent alignments to drift and become inaccurate.

Alignment Example 3, Japanese

This example demonstrates Phonsa's alignment capabilities on Japanese singing voice. Phonsa achieves superior alignment accuracy compared to MFA.

Alignment Example 4, Japanese

In this example, both Phonsa and MFA demonstrate good alignment results. However, Phonsa’s predicted breath segments (marked as '@') align well with the actual breath sounds visible in the Praat-generated spectrogram (where darker areas indicate higher energy and lighter areas indicate lower energy), even though these regions were not annotated in the ground truth. This discrepancy is one of the underlying reasons for the relatively low Bre F1 score on Japanese.

Task 2: Phoneme Segmentation Results

The figures below demonstrate Phonsa's ability to automatically segment phonemes from singing voice without lyrics. Phonsa can identify phoneme boundaries and classify phonemes directly from audio, enabling annotation when lyrics are unavailable. GT is the ground truth label. The sentence tier presents the phrase-level segmentation results derived from the phoneme segmentation output using a rule-based approach. This enables automatic splitting of a full song into multiple phrases during inference, which is beneficial for training singing voice synthesis (SVS) models.

Evaluating Annotation Utility via Singing Voice Synthesis

To demonstrate the practical utility of Phonsa annotations, we train FastSinger models using different annotation methods and compare their synthesis quality. The models are trained on the same dataset, and the only difference lies in the phoneme and pitch annotations used. Inference is conducted on the test set with ground truth annotations (unseen during training), using the same singers as in the training set. It is important to note that the Phonsa-generated annotations are obtained under the Task 2 phoneme segmentation setting, which does not rely on known lyrics during phoneme annotation. This demonstrates a more challenging setup compared to MFA's forced alignment. The audio samples below showcase how Phonsa-generated annotations lead to significantly improved naturalness and intelligibility in synthesized singing voice compared to traditional MFA-based annotations, approaching the quality achieved when training with ground-truth annotations.

Model Descriptions:

  • Vocoder (GT Mel): Reconstructs audio from ground truth mel-spectrogram using a vocoder.
  • FastSinger (GT): FastSinger with ground truth phoneme and pitch annotations.
  • FastSinger (MFA+ROSVOT): FastSinger with phoneme alignment from MFA and pitch predicted by ROSVOT.
  • FastSinger (Phonsa+ROSVOT): FastSinger with phoneme segmentation from our Phonsa and pitch predicted by ROSVOT.
